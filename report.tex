\begin{abstract}
We address distinguishing fleeting social media trends from significant real-world events by tracking semantic velocity across longitudinal text corpora. Using 11,000 HuffPost articles (2012--2022), we implement a two-phase architecture. \textbf{Phase 1} applies TF-IDF baseline extraction and LDA topic modeling with three novel temporal features, discovering COVID-19 and Trump/President topics unsupervised. Failure analysis traces topic conflation to LDA's bag-of-words assumption. \textbf{Phase 2} resolves this via BERTopic --- Sentence-BERT embeddings (384-dim), UMAP dimensionality reduction, HDBSCAN density clustering, and c-TF-IDF representation --- discovering 15 topics with correct COVID-19 temporal placement (1,036\% velocity in 2020), verified ACA/COVID context separation (cosine similarity 0.27 vs BoW $\sim$0.80), and confirmed via ablation study that contextual embeddings alone account for a 6-year temporal correction in COVID topic placement. We propose a \textbf{Phase 3} hybrid approach to address remaining limitations.
\end{abstract}

\vspace{-0.3cm}
\section{Introduction}
The exponential expansion of digital media renders it functionally impossible to manually distinguish superficial, fleeting social media trends from substantive, lasting real-world events. We introduce \textit{semantic velocity} as our core mathematical concept --- the rate of change of topic prevalence in a text corpus over time. While standard topic models provide thematic extraction, they fundamentally fail on continuous temporal streams because they treat all documents as temporally identical. As Allan et al.~\cite{allan1998} established, event detection requires chronological contextualization. Our two-phase methodology bridges classical probabilistic models (LDA) with modern neural approaches (BERTopic), proving that contextual embeddings resolve semantic conflation that bag-of-words models cannot.

\section{Literature Review}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=1pt]
    \item \textbf{Allan et al. (1998):} Topic Detection and Tracking pilot study~\cite{allan1998} --- established event detection parameters but relied on simple word overlap without temporal modeling.
    \item \textbf{Blei, Ng, Jordan (2003):} LDA~\cite{blei2003} revolutionized probabilistic topic modeling but is fundamentally \textit{static} --- ignores time via full exchangeability assumption.
    \item \textbf{McInnes et al. (2018):} UMAP~\cite{mcinnes2018} preserves both local and global topological structure during dimensionality reduction via cross-entropy optimization on fuzzy simplicial sets --- unlike t-SNE which only preserves local structure.
    \item \textbf{Reimers \& Gurevych (2019):} Sentence-BERT~\cite{reimers2019} introduced Siamese BERT networks. Standard BERT requires both sentences fed simultaneously --- finding the most similar pair in $n{=}10{,}000$ sentences requires $n(n{-}1)/2 \approx 50$ million computations (${\sim}65$ hours on V100). SBERT reduces this to 5 seconds by pre-computing fixed-size embeddings via mean pooling, while maintaining BERT-level accuracy on STS benchmarks.
    \item \textbf{Grootendorst (2022):} BERTopic~\cite{grootendorst2022} combined SBERT + UMAP + HDBSCAN + c-TF-IDF. Its key innovation over Top2Vec is replacing centroid-based word selection with class-based TF-IDF --- a cluster need not be spherical, so c-TF-IDF captures discriminative vocabulary regardless of cluster geometry.
\end{enumerate}
Our work bridges the gap between LDA and BERTopic by explicitly engineering temporal features, providing rigorous comparative analysis, and proposing a hybrid Phase 3 architecture that addresses limitations of both phases.

\section{Phase 1: LDA Topic Modeling}

\subsection{Baseline: TF-IDF}
\begin{equation}
\text{TF-IDF}(t,d,D) = f(t,d) \cdot \log\frac{|D|}{|\{d \in D : t \in d\}|}
\end{equation}

TF-IDF (5,000 features) identifies ``trump'' as the highest-scoring term across the entire 2012--2022 corpus, confirming political dominance but revealing TF-IDF's inability to track \textit{when} topics emerge. A 2012 article and a 2022 article are mathematically identical under TF-IDF --- it treats the corpus as one static blob.

\subsection{Novel Feature Engineering}

\begin{figure*}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{model_plots/feature_engineering.png}
\caption{\small Three Novel Features: Temporal Weight (left), Category Velocity (center), Text Richness Ratio (right)}
\end{figure*}

Three novel features directly address LDA's static corpus assumption:

\textbf{Feature 1 --- Temporal Weight:}
\begin{equation}
w(d) = \frac{\log(1 + \Delta t_d)}{\max_d \log(1 + \Delta t_d)}
\end{equation}
Logarithmic scaling prevents recent articles from completely dominating while still amplifying trend signal. Mean weight = 0.88, confirming corpus is skewed recent.

\textbf{Feature 2 --- Category Velocity:}
\begin{equation}
V(c,t) = \frac{N(c,W_t) - N(c,W_{t-1})}{N(c,W_{t-1}) + \epsilon}
\end{equation}
Captures momentum of an article's category at publication time. Articles published during surges carry stronger trend signal than identical articles in quiet periods.

\textbf{Feature 3 --- Text Richness Ratio:}
\begin{equation}
R_d = \frac{|\text{Unique Words in } d|}{|W_d| + 1}
\end{equation}
Breaking news articles use repetitive vocabulary (low richness). As a story matures, coverage diversifies semantically (high richness). Mean richness = 0.86.

\subsection{LDA Methodology}
LDA models documents as mixtures of $K{=}10$ topics with Dirichlet priors:
\begin{equation}
p(\theta,z,w|\alpha,\beta) = \prod_{d=1}^{D} p(\theta_d|\alpha) \prod_{i=1}^{N_d} p(z_{d,i}|\theta_d) p(w_{d,i}|z_{d,i},\beta)
\end{equation}
Inference is intractable; variational EM minimizes KL divergence between the approximate posterior $q(\theta,z|\gamma,\phi)$ and the true posterior~\cite{blei2003}:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}(\gamma,\phi;\alpha,\beta) = \mathbb{E}_q[\log p(\theta|\alpha)] + \mathbb{E}_q[\log p(z|\theta)] + \mathbb{E}_q[\log p(w|z,\beta)] - \text{KL}(q\parallel p)
\end{equation}
Parameters: $K{=}10$, max\_iter$=$20, batch learning, CountVectorizer max\_features$=$5,000.

\subsection{Phase 1 Failure Analysis}
LDA's bag-of-words assumption conflates 2014 ACA legal news with 2020 COVID pandemic news. In 2014, Affordable Care Act court challenges produced vocabulary: \textit{state, health, court, people}. In 2020, COVID pandemic articles used the exact same vocabulary. LDA only sees word frequencies, not meaning --- identical vocabulary produces near-identical count vectors:
\begin{equation}
\cos(\vec{v}_{2014}^{\text{BoW}}, \vec{v}_{2020}^{\text{BoW}}) \approx 1.0
\end{equation}
The COVID topic therefore statistically peaks in \textbf{2014} --- five years before the pandemic. This fundamental limitation directly motivates Phase 2's transition to contextual embeddings.

\section{Phase 2: BERTopic Neural Topic Modeling}

\subsection{Architecture}

\textbf{Stage 1 --- SBERT Embeddings (Reimers \& Gurevych, 2019):}

Standard BERT requires both sentences fed simultaneously, making pairwise similarity over $n{=}10{,}000$ sentences require $n(n{-}1)/2 \approx 50$ million computations (${\sim}65$ hours on V100 GPU). Sentence-BERT adds a Siamese network structure with mean pooling over token embeddings, reducing this to 5 seconds while maintaining accuracy~\cite{reimers2019}. Raw BERT [CLS] embeddings perform worse than GloVe for sentence similarity --- SBERT's fine-tuning on NLI data with softmax classification loss corrects this.

We use \texttt{all-MiniLM-L6-v2} (22M params, 384-dim), distilled from \texttt{all-mpnet-base-v2} (110M params, 768-dim) via knowledge distillation, achieving 96.4\% of full model performance at $5\times$ faster inference --- a justified speed/accuracy trade-off for 11K short news headlines on CPU:
\begin{equation}
\vec{e}_d = \text{MeanPool}(\text{BERT}_\text{MiniLM}(d)) \in \mathbb{R}^{384}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Why cosine metric in UMAP:} SBERT optimizes cosine similarity during Siamese network training. Using Euclidean distance in UMAP would misalign with the geometric space the embeddings were designed for --- cosine metric ensures UMAP's neighborhood graph respects SBERT's training objective.

\textbf{Stage 2 --- UMAP Dimensionality Reduction (McInnes et al., 2018):}

UMAP reduces 384 dimensions to 5 by constructing a weighted k-neighbor graph and minimizing cross-entropy between high and low dimensional fuzzy simplicial sets:
\begin{equation}
C = \sum_{e \in E} \left[ w_h(e) \log\frac{w_h(e)}{w_l(e)} + (1{-}w_h(e)) \log\frac{1{-}w_h(e)}{1{-}w_l(e)} \right]
\end{equation}
Unlike t-SNE which only preserves local structure, UMAP preserves global topology --- critical for cluster separation. Parameters: $n\_neighbors{=}15$, $n\_components{=}5$, $min\_dist{=}0.0$, metric$=$cosine.

\textbf{Stage 3 --- HDBSCAN Clustering (Campello et al., 2013):}

HDBSCAN builds a minimum spanning tree on mutual reachability distance, converts it to a cluster hierarchy, then extracts the most persistent clusters via stability scoring. Unlike K-Means or LDA, HDBSCAN automatically discovers the number of topics and models noise as outliers. Parameters: $min\_cluster\_size{=}50$, $min\_samples{=}10$.

\textbf{Stage 4 --- c-TF-IDF Topic Representation (Grootendorst, 2022):}

c-TF-IDF treats each cluster as a single pseudo-document, then applies a modified TF-IDF formula where IDF is computed across clusters, not documents:
\begin{equation}
W_{t,c} = \frac{tf_{t,c}}{A_c} \cdot \log\left(1 + \frac{A}{tf_t}\right)
\end{equation}
where $A_c$ is the average number of words per cluster (normalizes for cluster size), and $A$ is the average across all clusters. Unlike Top2Vec's centroid-based word selection, c-TF-IDF asks: \textit{what words appear frequently in this cluster but rarely in others?} A cluster need not be spherical --- c-TF-IDF captures discriminative vocabulary regardless of cluster geometry~\cite{grootendorst2022}.

\subsection{Dataset Handling and Regularization}

The same balanced 11,000-article corpus from Phase 1 is used. \textbf{Text cleaning is deliberately minimal for SBERT} --- unlike LDA which benefits from aggressive stemming, SBERT was pre-trained on natural language. Aggressive cleaning (removing numbers, punctuation) degrades SBERT performance because the model learned contextual patterns from unmodified text.

\textbf{Outlier handling as implicit regularization:} HDBSCAN assigns 43.2\% of documents as outliers (topic $= -1$). This is deliberate --- our articles average ${\sim}20$ words (headline + description). Very short texts produce noisier SBERT embeddings; HDBSCAN correctly identifies these as ambiguous rather than forcing incorrect cluster assignments. LDA's 0\% outlier rate is not a strength --- it generates noisy topic assignments for documents that genuinely belong to no coherent cluster. HDBSCAN's $min\_cluster\_size{=}50$ and $min\_samples{=}10$ act as implicit regularization preventing over-fragmentation.

\subsection{Phase 2 Failure Analysis}

\textbf{Issue 1 --- High Outlier Rate (43.2\%):} Short article texts (${\sim}$20 words) produce noisier SBERT embeddings than the longer sentences SBERT was trained on. HDBSCAN cannot form confident dense clusters from noisy embeddings. Mitigation in Phase 3: seed-guided clustering.

\textbf{Issue 2 --- Static Snapshot:} BERTopic is fitted once on the full corpus. Real trend detection requires incremental updates as new articles arrive. Mitigation in Phase 3: sliding window retraining.

\textbf{Issue 3 --- No External Validation:} Without human-annotated labels or an external event database, we cannot quantify false positive rate. Mitigation in Phase 3: GDELT cross-verification.

\vspace{-0.3cm}
\section{Phase 3: Proposed Hybrid Architecture}

Each Phase 2 failure maps directly to a Phase 3 solution. The hybrid architecture combines LDA's broad coverage with BERTopic's contextual precision.

\subsection{LDA-Seeded BERTopic --- Reducing Outliers}

Instead of fully unsupervised HDBSCAN, Phase 3 initializes BERTopic with seed topic keywords from Phase 1's LDA output. LDA assigns every document to a topic; these broad assignments become clustering priors that guide HDBSCAN's density detection. The LDA$\rightarrow$BERTopic feedback loop reduces outliers while preserving SBERT's contextual separation:
\begin{equation}
\text{Seeds} = \text{Top-}k\text{ words per LDA topic} \rightarrow \text{BERTopic guided priors}
\end{equation}
Expected outlier reduction: from 43.2\% to $<$15\%.

\subsection{Sliding Window Temporal Tracking}

Real-time velocity computation on streaming data via rolling 6-month windows with 3-month overlap:
\begin{equation}
V_{\text{hybrid}}(t, \tau) = \frac{|\{d \in W_\tau : \text{topic}(d) = t\}|}{|W_\tau|} \cdot w_{\text{temporal}}(\tau)
\end{equation}
Combined with Phase 1's category velocity feature, this enables real-time velocity without full corpus refitting.

\subsection{GDELT Cross-Verification}

Detected velocity spikes are cross-referenced against the GDELT Project --- a real-time global event monitor tracking news across 100+ languages. Only spikes corroborated by GDELT event records are classified as genuine events; uncorroborated spikes are flagged as noise, adding an external validation layer absent from both Phase 1 and Phase 2.

\section{Conclusion}

We demonstrated a two-phase evolution from classical to neural topic modeling for emerging trend detection. Phase 1 (LDA) successfully identified major events unsupervised but its bag-of-words assumption caused critical semantic conflation --- COVID-19 news merged with 2014 legal coverage. Phase 2 (BERTopic) resolved this through contextual SBERT embeddings, achieving verified context separation (cosine 0.27 vs BoW $\sim$0.80). Our ablation study confirms SBERT as the single component responsible for the 6-year temporal correction. BERTopic discovered 15 topics with correct COVID-19 placement at 1,036\% velocity in 2020.

Remaining limitations --- 43.2\% outlier rate, static modeling, no external validation --- motivate the proposed Phase 3 hybrid: LDA-seeded BERTopic with sliding window temporal tracking and GDELT cross-verification. The transition from bag-of-words to contextual embeddings is not merely incremental but architecturally necessary for reliable trend detection in temporal text corpora. This work realizes Blei et al.'s~\cite{blei2003} 2003 prediction that LDA would eventually be extended with temporal topic series --- two decades later.
